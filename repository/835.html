1. Separate lives

Presented byMargaret WardHistorian & author
In association withVoices 16BBC Northern Ireland

Its hard to believe that a love story could emerge from two of the most seismic events of 1916, the Easter Rising and the Battle of the Somme. The young Winifred Carney and George McBride, from Belfast, were embroiled in the violence of both. Their allegiances were different, but their hearts were eventually united.

In April 1916, Winnie was one of a small group of Irish Republicans who rebelled against British rule in Dublins Easter Rising. Just a few months later, George fought in support of those same British rulers at the Battle of the Somme.

Twelve years later, this most unlikely of couples would be married. But how did they beat the odds and find love?

4. Till death do us part

George McBride and Winifred Carney, pictured together as a married couple (image courtesy of Des Cassidy, the great nephew of Winifred Carney).

Those who knew the couple said each brought light into the others life. George softened Winnie and cajoled her out of some of her more hard-line thoughts. Neither changed their opinion of the Easter Rising. George thought it was folly and never understood the glorification of the rebels. For Winnie, it was a noble undertaking and her fellow fighters were heroes. But mutual respect and a shared compassion for people were stronger than any political differences.

And then there was their affection. In her letters, Winnie called George Mr Caveman. She pleaded with him to look after himself: You just dont know how much harm it does me if you are not well and sleeping all right." She looked forward with joy to their shared life together: I am just going to live now for all the things that you have promised me and all the places you are to take me.

Winnie was approaching 40 when she married George in 1928. He was 10 years younger. He was an atheist, she a devout Catholic.

Winnie died just 15 years later. She had probably suffered from latent TB contracted in her early youth. The couple had endured disapproval from friends and family, and even in death they suffered. Winnie was buried in an unmarked grave at Milltown Cemetery in Belfast because her brother refused to have the name McBride on her headstone, a final protest at her marriage to a Protestant. It was only in 1985 that a proper headstone was erected and she was honoured by her fellow republicans for her role in the Easter Rising.

A widower at 45, it is ironic that George was a former British soldier in receipt of an Irish rebels pension. He kept Winnie's memoirs safe and treasured the typewriter she used during the Easter Rising. Bereft, he never re-married and spent the last years of his life in the Ulster Volunteer Force Hospital, where he died in 1988. He continued to miss his wife: I hope I was worthy of her. She seemed to be so very happy with me.

Its awful being without Winnie.
